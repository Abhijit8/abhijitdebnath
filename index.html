Story of a Land

Badarpur is a small town in Assam, where many work as central government employees. Others own small businesses. The residents of Badarpur predominantly belong to the Hindu and Islamic communities . But almost all are Bengalees and speak Bangla as their native tongue. Most of the Bengali residents of Badarpur are migrants from East Bengal and settled here before partition and after partition between India and now Bangladesh . There has always been a hushed friction between Hindu and Muslim Bengali due to conflict of ideologies and ownership. Most of the residents fall under the lower and middle socio-economic class. Growing up I became more and more aware of the Political hypocrisy existed within this small town of Badarpur just like any other place. While there was mutual aversion between the Hindu and Muslims communities, both communities were aso fighting for their identity with the government.

Badarpur is situated on the river of Barak. The Barak River is split into two small rivers (kushiyara and Surma) and falls into Bangladesh. People have a lot of emotions tied up the Barak river as it is a part of the family to the communities . Through this journey I tried to capture some fleeting moments of daily life as it revolves around complex emotions that the people have towards their land, air and water. I believe the land lacked the sense of permanency since the colonial period. And this essence of uncertainty and local conflict makes them more aware about their identity. Amidst all this life goes on like a non-stop roller coaster.

With my works I attempted to create these fictional yet real frames of what is daily mundane life to many. There has been a play between the placements of objects and postures human figures to capture the order and chaos of life, which I first started capturing within my photography and worked its way into my production of works.

The Bomannahalli project
The Protected land

Bomannahalli is a part of south Bangalore, a busy, hustling and under various state and central govt. development projects. One of the major developments being the Namma Bengaluru Metro rail project. In order to create the expansion for the project the government reclaimed large chunks of land and many local Kanadiga owned small kirana stores, eateries were forced to evacuate at a price. While, the migration workers, started working the state sanctioned development of the same place. My curiosity sparked as, in a very short time the material landscape started changing into a structured commercial space. And the ideological landscape started shifting too with the dialogue that was being constructed with the people working the land and the land itself.

I used photography and site-specific installation with found material and painted bags
